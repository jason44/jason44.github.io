The laplacian is similar to the second derivative test in single-variable.
The second derivative is a measure of concavity.

  • When the graph is concave upwards, the surrounding first-derivatives are smaller (possibly negative) in the negative step and larger in the positive step (positive slope), thus the second derivative is positive.
  • Likewise, when the graph is concave down, the first-derivatives in the negative step is larger than in the positive step (negative slope), thus the second derivative is negative.

The second derivative is basically a measure of to what extent is this the case.
Obviously the second derivative is smallest when it is in a maximum and largest at a minimum.

This idea can be extended to the Laplacian.
Since is the divergence of the gradient, at points where is positive, vectors of the gradient near tend to point away from which indicates that the point may be close to or at a minimum.
(as if the point is at the bottom of a valley)

Where is negative, vectors near tend to point towards it (sink) which indicates that the point may be close to or at a maximum.

Thus, the Laplacian operator gives the difference between the average value of a function in the neighborhood of a point, and its value at that point.
